Standard deviations and variances

In addition to the average value, standard deviation and variance together are the second important parameters of a measurement series. The two values indicate how far the concrete values deviate on average from the average value. A standard deviation of 0 means that the measurement series consists of only one value. Negative standard deviations are not possible. The greater the standard deviation, the more the measured values of a measurement series vary.

In many cases it is very useful to consider the standard deviation of a series of measurements in addition to the average value, because when a series of measurements is compressed to only one parameter (the average value), too much information that would be interesting for the analysis is often lost.

Relationship between standard deviation and variance

The variance is the squared standard deviation or the standard deviation is the square root of the variance. When dealing analytically with probability distributions, the variance is usually easier to calculate than the standard deviation, so in stochastic formulas usually the variance is given first. From a practical point of view, the standard deviation is usually easier to understand.

Identifier

The standard deviation of the measurement series X is usually designated by Std[X] and the variance by Var[X].

Criticism

If different series of measurements are to be compared with regard to their fluctuations, standard deviation and variance can give a biased impression. These two quantities must always be considered in relation to the associated average value. If the service times of average 60 seconds fluctuate with a standard deviation of 30 seconds, this represents a greater fluctuation than service times of 3600 seconds which have a standard deviation of 40 seconds.

The coefficient of variation addresses exactly this problem. It provides a possibility to compare different measurement series directly with respect to their relative dispersion.

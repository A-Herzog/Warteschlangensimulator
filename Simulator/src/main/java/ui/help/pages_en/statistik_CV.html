Coefficients of variation

The coefficient of variation represents a normalized version of the standard deviation. Just like the standard deviation, the coefficient of variation never takes on values smaller than 0 and a value of 0 means that the measurement series consists of only one value.

Relationship with the standard deviation

In contrast to the standard deviation, the coefficient of variation is independent of the average value of the measurement series, so that different measurement series can be compared directly with regard to the strength of the variation via their coefficients of variation.

In concrete terms, the coefficient of variation is calculated as the quotient of the standard deviation and the average value.

Squared coefficient of variation

Besides the coefficient of variation, there is also the squared coefficient of variation. This is either obtained by squaring the coefficient of variation or by forming the quotient of the variance and the square of the average value.

Identifier

The coefficient of variation of the measurement series X is usually designated by CV[X] and the squared coefficient of variation by SCV[X].

Typical values

A coefficient of variation of 0 means that all values of the measurement series are identical. The measured values, which are based on an exponential distribution, have a coefficient of variation of 1.

In manufacturing processes, the coefficient of variation of service times usually depends on how much human interaction is required in the respective step. Typical values range from less than 0.3 for purely robotic manufacturing, to 0.3 to 0.6 for partially manual work steps, to values from 0.6 to over 1 for fully manual work steps.
